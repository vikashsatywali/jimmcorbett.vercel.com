About Jim Corbett

India's First & Most Prestigious National Park

Introduction

The Legacy of Jim Corbett

Jim Corbett National Park is Indiaâ€™s oldest and one of the most prestigious national parks, located in the beautiful state of Uttarakhand near Ramnagar. Known for its rich biodiversity, dense forests, and thriving population of Royal Bengal Tigers, the park attracts thousands of wildlife enthusiasts, photographers, and nature lovers every year.

Established in 1936 as Hailey National Park, it was later renamed in honor of the legendary conservationist Jim Corbett, who played a key role in wildlife protection and conservation in India.

Location & Geography

Nestled in the Himalayas

Jim Corbett National Park is situated in the Nainital district of Uttarakhand, nestled in the foothills of the Himalayas. The park is spread across approximately 520 square kilometers and is enriched by the Ramganga River, which enhances its scenic beauty and ecological diversity.

Our Heritage

History of Jim Corbett

The history of Jim Corbett National Park reflects Indiaâ€™s early efforts in wildlife conservation. The park continues to play a vital role in maintaining ecological balance and protecting our majestic wildlife.

  • 1936 Established as Hailey National Park, being the first national park in India.
  • 1957 Renamed as Jim Corbett National Park to honor the author and conservationist.
  • 1973 Became the first park to come under the prestigious Project Tiger initiative.

Expeditions

Safari Experience & Zones

One of the main attractions of Jim Corbett is its thrilling jungle safari experience. These safaris take you deep into the forest, offering a chance to witness wildlife in their natural habitat.

Types of Safari

Jeep Safari: A personalized standard experience entering the core and buffer zones.

Canter Safari: A communal open-bus safari exclusively operating inside the Dhikala zone.

Tip: Early morning and evening safaris provide the best opportunities for wildlife sightings, especially tigers.

The park is divided into multiple tourism zones, each differing in landscape, wildlife density, and safari experience:
